The Turing Language

Overview

In this post we'll have a look at what's known as variational inference (VI), a family of approximate Bayesian inference methods. In particular, we will focus on one of the more standard VI methods called Automatic Differentiation Variational Inference (ADVI).

Here we'll have a look at the theory behind VI, but if you're interested in how to use ADVI in Turing.jl, check out this tutorial.

Motivation

In Bayesian inference one usually specifies a model as follows: given data xii=1n\\{ x_i\\}_{i = 1}^nxi​i=1n​,

prior:z∼p(z)likelihood:xi∼i.i.d.p(x∣z)wherei=1,…,n \begin{align*} \text{prior:} \quad z &\sim p(z) \\\\ \text{likelihood:} \quad x_i &\overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} p(x \mid z) \quad \text{where} \quad i = 1, \dots, n \end{align*} prior:zlikelihood:xi​​∼p(z)∼i.i.d.p(x∣z)wherei=1,…,n​

where ∼i.i.d.\overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim}∼i.i.d. denotes that the samples are identically independently distributed. Our goal in Bayesian inference is then to find the posterior

p(z∣{xi}i=1n)=∏i=1np(z∣xi). p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} p(z \mid x_i). p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​)=i=1∏n​p(z∣xi​).

In general one cannot obtain a closed form expression for <span>p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n)</span>, but one might still be able to sample from p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) with guarantees of converging to the target posterior p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) as the number of samples go to ∞\infty∞, e.g. MCMC.

As you are hopefully already aware, Turing.jl provides a lot of different methods with asymptotic exactness guarantees that we can apply to such a problem!

Unfortunately, these unbiased samplers can be prohibitively expensive to run. As the model ppp increases in complexity, the convergence of these unbiased samplers can slow down dramatically. Still, in the infinite limit, these methods should converge to the true posterior! But infinity is fairly large, like, at least more than 12, so this might take a while.

In such a case it might be desirable to sacrifice some of these asymptotic guarantees, and instead approximate the posterior p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) using some other model which we'll denote q(z)q(z)q(z).

There are multiple approaches to take in this case, one of which is variational inference (VI).

Variational Inference (VI)

In VI, we're looking to approximate p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n )p(z∣xi​_i=1n) using some approximate or variational posterior q(z)q(z)q(z).

To approximate something you need a notion of what "close" means. In the context of probability densities a standard such "measure" of closeness is the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence , though this is far from the only one. The KL-divergence is defined between two densities q(z)q(z)q(z) and p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) as

DKL(q(z),p(z∣{xi}i=1n))=∫log⁡(q(z)∏i=1np(z∣xi))q(z)dz=Ez∼q(z)[log⁡q(z)−∑i=1nlog⁡p(z∣xi)]=Ez∼q(z)[log⁡q(z)]−∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(z∣xi)]. \begin{align*} \mathrm{D_{KL}} \left( q(z), p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) \right) &= \int \log \left( \frac{q(z)}{\prod_{i = 1}^n p(z \mid x_i)} \right) q(z) \mathrm{d}{z} \\\\ &= \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log q(z) - \sum_{i = 1}^n \log p(z \mid x_i) \right] \\\\ &= \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log q(z) \right] - \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(z \mid x_i) \right]. \end{align*} DKL​(q(z),p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​))​=∫log(∏i=1n​p(z∣xi​)q(z)​)q(z)dz=Ez∼q(z)​[logq(z)−i=1∑n​logp(z∣xi​)]=Ez∼q(z)​[logq(z)]−i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(z∣xi​)].​

It's worth noting that unfortunately the KL-divergence is not a metric/distance in the analysis-sense due to its lack of symmetry. On the other hand, it turns out that minimizing the KL-divergence that it's actually equivalent to maximizing the log-likelihood! Also, under reasonable restrictions on the densities at hand,

DKL(q(z),p(z∣{xi}i=1n))=0  ⟺  q(z)=p(z∣{xi}i=1n),∀z. \mathrm{D_{KL}}\left(q(z), p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) \right) = 0 \quad \iff \quad q(z) = p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n), \quad \forall z. DKL​(q(z),p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​))=0⟺q(z)=p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​),∀z.

Therefore one could (and we will) attempt to approximate p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) using a density q(z)q(z)q(z) by minimizing the KL-divergence between these two!

One can also show that DKL≥0\mathrm{D_{KL}} \ge 0DKL​≥0, which we'll need later. Finally notice that the KL-divergence is only well-defined when in fact q(z)q(z)q(z) is zero everywhere p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) is zero, i.e.

supp(q(z))⊆supp(p(z∣x)). \mathrm{supp}\left(q(z)\right) \subseteq \mathrm{supp}\left(p(z \mid x)\right). supp(q(z))⊆supp(p(z∣x)).

Otherwise, there might be a point z0∼q(z)z_0 \sim q(z)z0​∼q(z) such that p(z0∣xi_i=1n)=0p(z_0 \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n) = 0p(z0​∣xi​_i=1n)=0, resulting in log⁡(q(z)0)\log\left(\frac{q(z)}{0}\right)log(0q(z)​) which doesn't make sense!

One major problem: as we can see in the definition of the KL-divergence, we need p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) for any zzz if we want to compute the KL-divergence between this and q(z)q(z)q(z). We don't have that. The entire reason we even do Bayesian inference is that we don't know the posterior! Cleary this isn't going to work. Or is it?!

Computing KL-divergence without knowing the posterior

First off, recall that

p(z∣xi)=p(xi,z)p(xi) p(z \mid x_i) = \frac{p(x_i, z)}{p(x_i)} p(z∣xi​)=p(xi​)p(xi​,z)​

so we can write

DKL(q(z),p(z∣{xi}i=1n))=Ez∼q(z)[log⁡q(z)]−∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi,z)−log⁡p(xi)]=Ez∼q(z)[log⁡q(z)]−∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi,z)]+∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi)]=Ez∼q(z)[log⁡q(z)]−∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi,z)]+∑i=1nlog⁡p(xi), \begin{align*} \mathrm{D_{KL}} \left( q(z), p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) \right) &= \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log q(z) \right] - \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i, z) - \log p(x_i) \right] \\\\ &= \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log q(z) \right] - \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i, z) \right] + \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i) \right] \\\\ &= \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log q(z) \right] - \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i, z) \right] + \sum_{i = 1}^n \log p(x_i), \end{align*} DKL​(q(z),p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​))​=Ez∼q(z)​[logq(z)]−i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​,z)−logp(xi​)]=Ez∼q(z)​[logq(z)]−i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​,z)]+i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​)]=Ez∼q(z)​[logq(z)]−i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​,z)]+i=1∑n​logp(xi​),​

where in the last equality we used the fact that p(xi)p(x_i)p(xi​) is independent of zzz.

Now you're probably thinking "Oh great! Now you've introduced p(xi)p(x_i)p(xi​) which we also can't compute (in general)!". Woah. Calm down human. Let's do some more algebra. The above expression can be rearranged to

DKL(q(z),p(z∣{xi}i=1n))+∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi,z)]−Ez∼q(z)[log⁡q(z)]⏟=:ELBO(q)=∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi)]⏟constant. \mathrm{D_{KL}} \left( q(z), p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) \right) + \underbrace{\sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i, z) \right] - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log q(z) \right]}_{=: \mathrm{ELBO}(q)} = \underbrace{\sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i) \right]}_{\text{constant}}. DKL​(q(z),p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​))+=:ELBO(q)i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​,z)]−Ez∼q(z)​[logq(z)]​​=constanti=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​)]​​.

See? The left-hand side is constant and, as we mentioned before, D_KL≥0\mathrm{D\_{KL}} \ge 0D_KL≥0. What happens if we try to maximize the term we just gave the completely arbitrary name ELBO\mathrm{ELBO}ELBO? Well, if ELBO\mathrm{ELBO}ELBO goes up while p(xi)p(x_i)p(xi​) stays constant then D_KL\mathrm{D\_{KL}}D_KLhas to go down! That is, the q(z)q(z)q(z) which minimizes the KL-divergence is the same q(z)q(z)q(z) which maximizes ELBO(q)\mathrm{ELBO}(q)ELBO(q):

argminq DKL(q(z),p(z∣{xi}i=1n))=argmaxq ELBO(q) \underset{q}{\mathrm{argmin}} \ \mathrm{D_{KL}} \left( q(z), p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) \right) = \underset{q}{\mathrm{argmax}} \ \mathrm{ELBO}(q) qargmin​ DKL​(q(z),p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​))=qargmax​ ELBO(q)

where

ELBO(q):=(∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi,z)])−Ez∼q(z)[log⁡q(z)]=(∑i=1nEz∼q(z)[log⁡p(xi,z)])+H(q(z)) \begin{align*} \mathrm{ELBO}(q) &:= \left( \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i, z) \right] \right) - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log q(z) \right] \\\\ &= \left( \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i, z) \right] \right) + \mathbb{H}\left( q(z) \right) \end{align*} ELBO(q)​:=(i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​,z)])−Ez∼q(z)​[logq(z)]=(i=1∑n​Ez∼q(z)​[logp(xi​,z)])+H(q(z))​

and H(q(z))\mathbb{H} \left(q(z) \right)H(q(z)) denotes the (differential) entropy of q(z)q(z)q(z).

Assuming joint p(xi,z)p(x_i, z)p(xi​,z) and the entropy H(q(z))\mathbb{H}\left(q(z)\right)H(q(z)) are both tractable, we can use a Monte-Carlo for the remaining expectation. This leaves us with the following tractable expression

argminq DKL(q(z),p(z∣{xi}i=1n))≈argmaxq ELBO^(q) \underset{q}{\mathrm{argmin}} \ \mathrm{D_{KL}} \left( q(z), p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) \right) \approx \underset{q}{\mathrm{argmax}} \ \widehat{\mathrm{ELBO}}(q) qargmin​ DKL​(q(z),p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​))≈qargmax​ ELBO(q)

where

ELBO^(q)=1m(∑k=1m∑i=1nlog⁡p(xi,zk))+H(q(z))wherezk∼q(z)∀k=1,…,m. \widehat{\mathrm{ELBO}}(q) = \frac{1}{m} \left( \sum_{k = 1}^m \sum_{i = 1}^n \log p(x_i, z_k) \right) + \mathbb{H} \left(q(z)\right) \quad \text{where} \quad z_k \sim q(z) \quad \forall k = 1, \dots, m. Hence, as long as we can sample from q(z)q(z)q(z) somewhat efficiently, we can indeed minimize the KL-divergence! Neat, eh?

Sidenote: in the case where q(z)q(z)q(z) is tractable but H(q(z))\mathbb{H} \left(q(z) \right)H(q(z)) is not , we can use an Monte-Carlo estimate for this term too but this generally results in a higher-variance estimate.

Also, I fooled you real good: the ELBO isn't an arbitrary name, hah! In fact it's an abbreviation for the expected lower bound (ELBO) because it, uhmm, well, it's the expected lower bound (remember DKL≥0\mathrm{D_{KL}} \ge 0DKL​≥0). Yup.

Maximizing the ELBO

Finding the optimal qqq over all possible densities of course isn't feasible. Instead we consider a family of parameterized densities D_Θ\mathscr{D}\_{\Theta}D_Θ where Θ\ThetaΘ denotes the space of possible parameters. Each density in this family qθ∈D_Θq_{\theta} \in \mathscr{D}\_{\Theta}qθ​∈D_Θ is parameterized by a unique θ∈Θ\theta \in \Thetaθ∈Θ. Moreover, we'll assume

  1. q_θ(z)q\_{\theta}(z)q_θ(z), i.e. evaluating the probability density qqq at any point zzz, is differentiable

  2. z∼qθ(z)z \sim q_{\theta}(z)z∼qθ​(z), i.e. the process of sampling from qθ(z)q_{\theta}(z)qθ​(z), is differentiable

(1) is fairly straight-forward, but (2) is a bit tricky. What does it even mean for a sampling process to be differentiable? This is quite an interesting problem in its own right and would require something like a 50-page paper to properly review the different approaches (highly recommended read).

We're going to make use of a particular such approach which goes under a bunch of different names: reparametrization trick, path derivative, etc. This refers to making the assumption that all elements q_θ∈Q_Θq\_{\theta} \in \mathscr{Q}\_{\Theta}q_θ∈Q_Θ can be considered as reparameterizations of some base density, say qˉ(z)\bar{q}(z)qˉ​(z). That is, if q_θ∈Q_Θq\_{\theta} \in \mathscr{Q}\_{\Theta}q_θ∈Q_Θ then

z∼qθ(z)  ⟺  z:=gθ(z~)wherezˉ∼qˉ(z) z \sim q_{\theta}(z) \quad \iff \quad z := g_{\theta}(\tilde{z}) \quad \text{where} \quad \bar{z} \sim \bar{q}(z) z∼qθ​(z)⟺z:=gθ​(z~)wherezˉ∼qˉ​(z)

for some function g_θg\_{\theta}g_θ differentiable wrt. θ\thetaθ. So all q_θ∈Q_Θq\_{\theta} \in \mathscr{Q}\_{\Theta}q_θ∈Q_Θ are using the same reparameterization-function ggg but each q_θq\_{\theta}q_θ correspond to different choices of θ\thetaθ for f_θf\_{\theta}f_θ.

Under this assumption we can differentiate the sampling process by taking the derivative of gθg_{\theta}gθ​ wrt. θ\thetaθ, and thus we can differentiate the entire ELBO^(qθ)\widehat{\mathrm{ELBO}}(q_{\theta})ELBO(qθ​) wrt. θ\thetaθ! With the gradient available we can either try to solve for optimality either by setting the gradient equal to zero or maximize ELBO^(qθ)\widehat{\mathrm{ELBO}}(q_{\theta})ELBO(qθ​) stepwise by traversing QΘ\mathscr{Q}_{\Theta}QΘ​ in the direction of steepest ascent. For the sake of generality, we're going to go with the stepwise approach.

With all this nailed down, we eventually reach the section on Automatic Differentiation Variational Inference (ADVI).

Automatic Differentiation Variational Inference (ADVI)

So let's revisit the assumptions we've made at this point:

  1. The variational posterior q_θq\_{\theta}q_θ is in a parameterized family of densities denoted Q_Θ\mathscr{Q}\_{\Theta}Q_Θ, with θ∈Θ\theta \in \Thetaθ∈Θ.

  2. Q_Θ\mathscr{Q}\_{\Theta}Q_Θ is a space of reparameterizable densities with qˉ(z)\bar{q}(z)qˉ​(z) as the base-density.

  3. The parameterization function g_θg\_{\theta}g_θ is differentiable wrt. θ\thetaθ.

  4. Evaluation of the probability density q_θ(z)q\_{\theta}(z)q_θ(z) is differentiable wrt. θ\thetaθ.

  5. H(qθ(z))\mathbb{H}\left(q_{\theta}(z)\right)H(qθ​(z)) is tractable.

  6. Evaluation of the joint density p(x,z)p(x, z)p(x,z) is tractable and differentiable wrt. zzz

  7. The support of p(z∣x)p(z \mid x)p(z∣x) is a subspace of the support of q(z)q(z)q(z): supp(p(z∣x))⊆supp(q(z))\mathrm{supp}\left(p(z \mid x)\right) \subseteq \mathrm{supp}\left(q(z)\right)supp(p(z∣x))⊆supp(q(z)).

All of these are not necessary to do VI, but they are very convenient and results in a fairly flexible approach. One distribution which has a density satisfying all of the above assumptions except (7) (we'll get back to this in second) for any tractable and differentiable p(z∣{xi}i=1n)p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​) is the good ole' Gaussian/normal distribution:

z∼N(μ,Σ)  ⟺  z=gμ,L(zˉ):=μ+LTz~wherezˉ∼qˉ(z):=N(1d,Id×d) z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma) \quad \iff \quad z = g_{\mu, L}(\bar{z}) := \mu + L^T \tilde{z} \quad \text{where} \quad \bar{z} \sim \bar{q}(z) := \mathcal{N}(1_d, I_{d \times d}) z∼N(μ,Σ)⟺z=gμ,L​(zˉ):=μ+LTz~wherezˉ∼qˉ​(z):=N(1d​,Id×d​)

where Σ=LLT\Sigma = L L^TΣ=LLT, with LLL obtained from the Cholesky-decomposition. Abusing notation a bit, we're going to write

θ=(μ,Σ):=(μ1,…,μd,L11,…,L1,d,L2,1,…,L2,d,…,Ld,1,…,Ld,d). \theta = (\mu, \Sigma) := (\mu_1, \dots, \mu_d, L_{11}, \dots, L_{1, d}, L_{2, 1}, \dots, L_{2, d}, \dots, L_{d, 1}, \dots, L_{d, d}). θ=(μ,Σ):=(μ1​,…,μd​,L11​,…,L1,d​,L2,1​,…,L2,d​,…,Ld,1​,…,Ld,d​).

With this assumption we finally have a tractable expression for ELBO^(q_μ,Σ)\widehat{\mathrm{ELBO}}(q\_{\mu, \Sigma})ELBO(q_μ,Σ)! Well, assuming (7) is holds. Since a Gaussian has non-zero probability on the entirety of Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd, we also require p(z∣xi_i=1n)p(z \mid \\{ x_i \\}\_{i = 1}^n)p(z∣xi​_i=1n) to have non-zero probability on all of Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd.

Though not necessary, we'll often make a mean-field assumption for the variational posterior q(z)q(z)q(z), i.e. assume independence between the latent variables. In this case, we'll write

θ=(μ,σ2):=(μ1,…,μd,σ12,…,σd2). \theta = (\mu, \sigma^2) := (\mu_1, \dots, \mu_d, \sigma_1^2, \dots, \sigma_d^2). θ=(μ,σ2):=(μ1​,…,μd​,σ12​,…,σd2​).

supp(q(s,m))⊈supp(p(z∣{xi}i=1n)). \mathrm{supp} \left( q(s, m) \right) \not\subseteq \mathrm{supp} \left( p(z \mid \{ x_i \}_{i = 1}^n) \right). supp(q(s,m))⊆supp(p(z∣{xi​}i=1n​)).

Recall from the definition of the KL-divergence that when this is the case, the KL-divergence isn't well defined. This gets us to the automatic part of ADVI.

"Automatic"? How?

For a lot of the standard (continuous) densities ppp we can actually construct a probability density p~\tilde{p}p~​ with non-zero probability on all of R\mathbb{R}R by transforming the "constrained" probability density ppp to p~\tilde{p}p~​. In fact, in these cases this is a one-to-one relationship. As we'll see, this helps solve the support-issue we've been going on and on about.

Transforming densities using change of variables

If we want to compute the probability of xxx taking a value in some set A⊆supp(p(x))A \subseteq \mathrm{supp} \left( p(x) \right)A⊆supp(p(x)), we have to integrate p(x)p(x)p(x) over AAA, i.e.

Pp(x∈A)=∫Ap(x)dx. \mathbb{P}_p(x \in A) = \int_A p(x) \mathrm{d}x. Pp​(x∈A)=∫A​p(x)dx.

This means that if we have a differentiable bijection f:supp(q(x))→Rdf: \mathrm{supp} \left( q(x) \right) \to \mathbb{R}^df:supp(q(x))→Rd with differentiable inverse f−1:Rd→supp(p(x))f^{-1}: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathrm{supp} \left( p(x) \right)f−1:Rd→supp(p(x)), we can perform a change of variables

Pp(x∈A)=∫f−1(A)p(f−1(y)) ∣det⁡Jf−1(y)∣ dy, \mathbb{P}_p(x \in A) = \int_{f^{-1}(A)} p \left(f^{-1}(y) \right) \ \left| \det \mathcal{J}_{f^{-1}}(y) \right| \ \mathrm{d}y, Pp​(x∈A)=∫f−1(A)​p(f−1(y)) ∣∣​detJf−1​(y)∣∣​ dy,

where J_f−1(x)\mathcal{J}\_{f^{-1}}(x)J_f−1(x) denotes the jacobian of f−1f^{-1}f−1 evaluted at xxx. Observe that this defines a probability distribution

Pp~(y∈f−1(A))=∫f−1(A)p~(y)dy, \mathbb{P}_{\tilde{p}}\left(y \in f^{-1}(A) \right) = \int_{f^{-1}(A)} \tilde{p}(y) \mathrm{d}y, Pp~​​(y∈f−1(A))=∫f−1(A)​p~​(y)dy,

since f−1(supp(p(x)))=Rdf^{-1}\left(\mathrm{supp} (p(x)) \right) = \mathbb{R}^df−1(supp(p(x)))=Rd which has probability 1. This probability distribution has density p~(y)\tilde{p}(y)p~​(y) with supp(p~(y))=Rd\mathrm{supp} \left( \tilde{p}(y) \right) = \mathbb{R}^dsupp(p~​(y))=Rd, defined

p~(y)=p(f−1(y)) ∣det⁡Jf−1(y)∣ \tilde{p}(y) = p \left( f^{-1}(y) \right) \ \left| \det \mathcal{J}_{f^{-1}}(y) \right| p~​(y)=p(f−1(y)) ∣∣​detJf−1​(y)∣∣​

or equivalently

p~(f(x))=p(x)∣det⁡Jf(x)∣ \tilde{p} \left( f(x) \right) = \frac{p(x)}{\big| \det \mathcal{J}_{f}(x) \big|} p~​(f(x))=∣∣​detJf​(x)∣∣​p(x)​

due to the fact that

∣det⁡Jf−1(y)∣=∣det⁡Jf(x)∣−1 \big| \det \mathcal{J}_{f^{-1}}(y) \big| = \big| \det \mathcal{J}_{f}(x) \big|^{-1} ∣∣​detJf−1​(y)∣∣​=∣∣​detJf​(x)∣∣​−1

Note: it's also necessary that the log-abs-det-jacobian term is non-vanishing. This can for example be accomplished by assuming fff to also be elementwise monotonic.

Back to VI

So why is this is useful? Well, we're looking to generalize our approach using a normal distribution to cases where the supports don't match up. How about defining q(z)q(z)q(z) by

η∼N(μ,Σ),z=f−1(η), \begin{align*} \eta &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma), \\\\ z &= f^{-1}(\eta), \end{align*} ηz​∼N(μ,Σ),=f−1(η),​

where f−1:Rd→supp(p(z∣x))f^{-1}: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathrm{supp} \left( p(z \mid x) \right)f−1:Rd→supp(p(z∣x)) is a differentiable bijection with differentiable inverse. Then z∼q_μ,Σ(z)  ⟹  z∈supp(p(z∣x))z \sim q\_{\mu, \Sigma}(z) \implies z \in \mathrm{supp} \left( p(z \mid x) \right)z∼q_μ,Σ(z)⟹z∈supp(p(z∣x)) as we wanted. The resulting variational density is

qμ,Σ(z)=pN(μ,Σ)(f(z)) ∣det⁡Jf(z)∣. q_{\mu, \Sigma}(z) = p_{\mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)}\left( f(z) \right) \ \big| \det \mathcal{J}_{f}(z) \big|. qμ,Σ​(z)=pN(μ,Σ)​(f(z)) ∣∣​detJf​(z)∣∣​.

Note that the way we've constructed q(z)q(z)q(z) here is basically a reverse of the approach we described above. Here we sample from a distribution with support on R\mathbb{R}R and transform to supp(p(z∣x))\mathrm{supp} \left( p(z \mid x) \right)supp(p(z∣x)).

If we want to write the ELBO explicitly in terms of η\etaη rather than zzz, the first term in the ELBO becomes

Ez∼qμ,Σ(z)[log⁡p(xi,z)]=Eη∼N(μ,Σ)[log⁡p(xi,f−1(η))∣det⁡Jf−1(η)∣]=Eη∼N(μ,Σ)[log⁡p(xi,f−1(η))]−Eη∼N(μ,Σ)[∣det⁡Jf−1(η)∣]. \begin{align*} \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_{\mu, \Sigma}(z)} \left[ \log p(x_i, z) \right] &= \mathbb{E}_{\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)} \Bigg[ \log \frac{p\left(x_i, f^{-1}(\eta) \right)}{\big| \det \mathcal{J}_{f^{-1}}(\eta) \big|} \Bigg] \\\\ &= \mathbb{E}_{\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)} \left[ \log p\left(x_i, f^{-1}(\eta) \right) \right] - \mathbb{E}_{\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)} \left[ \left| \det \mathcal{J}_{f^{-1}}(\eta) \right| \right]. \end{align*} Ez∼qμ,Σ​(z)​[logp(xi​,z)]​=Eη∼N(μ,Σ)​[log∣∣​detJf−1​(η)∣∣​p(xi​,f−1(η))​]=Eη∼N(μ,Σ)​[logp(xi​,f−1(η))]−Eη∼N(μ,Σ)​[∣∣​detJf−1​(η)∣∣​].​

The entropy is invariant under change of variables, thus H(q_μ,Σ(z))\mathbb{H} \left(q\_{\mu, \Sigma}(z)\right)H(q_μ,Σ(z)) is simply the entropy of the normal distribution which is known analytically.
